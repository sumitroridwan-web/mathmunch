Page 2 · The Grid System
Captain Cora and her co-navigator Nate were the most celebrated explorers in the Maritime Academy of Numerica. They had sailed every ocean, crossed every desert, and charted every mountain range in the known world. But when the Academy's priceless collection of mathematical artefacts went missing, scattered across nine secret grid locations, only Cora and Nate possessed the skill to retrieve them.

"Every location in this world," Cora explained, spreading a vast coordinate map across the navigation table, "can be identified precisely using just two numbers — a coordinate pair written as (x, y)."

"The x-coordinate tells you how far to travel across — along the horizontal axis. The y-coordinate tells you how far to travel up — along the vertical axis." Nate traced the path to point (3, 2): three steps right, then two steps up.

"Remember this rule always," Cora said firmly. "Along the corridor, then up the stairs. Across first, then up. The x-axis is always the first number, the y-axis always the second."

Nate marked the point precisely on the map. Their mission to recover the nine artefacts was about to begin.
Every coordinate is an address in space. Get the order wrong — x then y — and you'll navigate to entirely the wrong location. Precision is everything on this mission.
📍 Coordinates (x, y)

COORDINATES (x, y): x comes first (across), y comes second (up). Remember: "along the corridor, then up the stairs!" (3, 2) means 3 across, 2 up.

Page 3
Five artefacts hidden at secret coordinates
Page 3 · Reading Coordinates
The Academy's intelligence report identified five artefact locations on the first map. Each artefact was marked with a symbol, and each symbol had a coordinate address. Cora and Nate's task was to read each coordinate precisely and navigate to it.

"Reading a coordinate is a systematic process," Nate explained, pointing to the map's origin — the point (0, 0) where both axes meet. "You always begin at the origin. From there, count rightward along the x-axis to reach the x-value. Then, without moving backward, count upward along the y-axis to reach the y-value."

"Consider the golden star on this map. Starting from the origin, I count 1 unit right and 3 units up — giving me the coordinate (1, 3)." Cora confirmed this reading with a precise compass measurement.

"The diamond is at (4, 6): four right, six up. The ancient vase is at (7, 2): seven right, two up. The scroll is at (3, 5): three right, five up. And the sword is at (6, 4): six right, four up."

Each coordinate was unique — no two artefacts occupied the same location. In a coordinate system, every point has one and only one address.
🗺️ Reading Coordinates

To READ a coordinate: start at the origin (0, 0). Count RIGHT for x, then count UP for y. Write as (x, y).

Page 4 · Plotting Coordinates
Reading coordinates from a map was one skill; plotting coordinates — placing a new point on a blank grid — was the complementary skill that Nate now needed to master for their mission. The Academy had given them new intelligence: a second artefact was at location (4, 5), but it had not yet been marked on the navigation chart.

"Plotting follows four precise steps," Cora explained, producing a clean grid. "Step 1: begin at the origin — the point (0, 0) where the axes cross. Step 2: move right along the x-axis by the x-value. In this case, move 4 units right. Step 3: from that position, move up parallel to the y-axis by the y-value — 5 units up. Step 4: mark the point with a clear dot and label it."

Nate followed each step methodically, drawing the path in red then green, finally marking the point with a bold dot at the intersection. "The key is to move horizontally first, then vertically," Nate observed. "Doing it in reverse — up first, then right — would bring you to the same spot, but the standard convention is x first."

"Consistency prevents navigation errors," Cora agreed. "In real-world navigation and cartography, everyone must follow the same convention or maps become useless."
Worked Example: Plot (4, 5)
1Start at the origin (0, 0)
2Move 4 units right along the x-axis
3Move 5 units up parallel to the y-axis
4Mark the point and label it (4, 5)
✏️ Plotting Coordinates

To PLOT (4, 5): Step 1: Start at origin. Step 2: Move 4 right. Step 3: Move 5 up. Step 4: Mark the point.

Page 5 · Shapes on Grids
The third artefact was hidden within an enclosed region on the grid — a rectangular zone whose four corners were specified by coordinates. Cora and Nate would need to plot these corner points and connect them to determine the shape and find the artefact within it.

"When a shape is defined by its vertices — its corner points — we plot each vertex as a coordinate, then connect them with straight lines in order," Cora explained. "The rectangle has four corners: (1, 1), (5, 1), (5, 3), and (1, 3)."

Nate plotted each point methodically, then connected them. A clear rectangle appeared on the grid. "The bottom side runs from (1, 1) to (5, 1) — both y-values equal 1, so this is a horizontal line. The right side runs from (5, 1) to (5, 3) — both x-values equal 5, so this is a vertical line."

"We can calculate the dimensions from the coordinates: width = 5 − 1 = 4 units. Height = 3 − 1 = 2 units. Area = 4 × 2 = 8 square units." Cora confirmed the artefact was inside the rectangle.

Coordinate geometry allows mathematicians to describe shapes algebraically as well as visually — a powerful connection between geometry and algebra.
🔷 Shapes from Coordinates

To draw a shape: plot each vertex (corner), then connect them with straight lines in order. Check the shape looks right — parallel sides, right angles, etc.

Average each coordinate to find the middle point
Page 6 · Midpoints
The fourth artefact was not at a grid point itself — the intelligence report indicated it was located exactly halfway between two marked waypoints. Cora immediately recognised this as a midpoint calculation problem.

"The midpoint of two coordinate points is the point exactly halfway between them," Cora explained, marking the two waypoints on the chart. "To calculate it, we find the average of the x-coordinates and the average of the y-coordinates separately."

"The midpoint formula is: Midpoint = ((x₁ + x₂)/2, (y₁ + y₂)/2). For the waypoints (2, 4) and (6, 4): x-midpoint = (2 + 6)/2 = 8/2 = 4. y-midpoint = (4 + 4)/2 = 8/2 = 4. Midpoint = (4, 4)."

"Notice that when the y-coordinates are equal, the midpoint lies on the same horizontal line — which makes intuitive sense." Nate plotted the midpoint carefully. "Let us verify with a second example: waypoints (1, 3) and (5, 7). x-midpoint = (1 + 5)/2 = 3. y-midpoint = (3 + 7)/2 = 5. Midpoint = (3, 5)."

Nate confirmed visually that (3, 5) appeared to be equidistant from both waypoints. The artefact was recovered.
⬛ Midpoints

MIDPOINT of two points: average each coordinate. Midpoint of (2, 4) and (6, 4) = ((2+6)/2, (4+4)/2) = (4, 4).

Page 7
Every point moves by the same vector (4 right, 2 up)
Page 7 · Translations
The sixth artefact presented a more complex challenge. Intelligence indicated that it had been moved — translated — from its original location by an unknown displacement. Cora and Nate knew the original position of the artefact's container and the translation vector. Their task was to calculate the new position.

"A translation is a transformation that slides a point or shape from one position to another, without any rotation, reflection, or change in size," Cora explained. "Every point in the shape moves by exactly the same amount, in exactly the same direction."

"This movement is described by a translation vector — for example, '4 right and 2 up'. To apply it, we add 4 to every x-coordinate and add 2 to every y-coordinate." Nate worked through the triangle: original vertices (1, 1), (3, 1), and (2, 3). After the translation: (1+4, 1+2) = (5, 3); (3+4, 1+2) = (7, 3); (2+4, 3+2) = (6, 5).

"The shape is identical in size and orientation — it has simply been repositioned. No vertex has moved more or less than any other." Nate confirmed the translated triangle on the chart and located the artefact at position (6, 5).
➡️ Translations

TRANSLATION: slide a shape without rotating or flipping. Add the same vector to every coordinate. Move 4 right, 2 up: every x+4, every y+2.

Reflecting changes the sign of one coordinate
Page 8 · Reflections
The seventh artefact had been hidden using a mirror transformation — a reflection across one of the grid axes. To find it, Cora and Nate needed to understand exactly how reflections alter coordinates.

"A reflection produces a mirror image of a point or shape across a given line," Cora explained. "The reflected image is the same distance from the mirror line as the original, but on the opposite side."

"When reflecting across the y-axis: the x-coordinate changes sign (positive becomes negative, negative becomes positive), and the y-coordinate remains unchanged. Original point (3, 2) → reflected point (−3, 2)."

"When reflecting across the x-axis: the y-coordinate changes sign, and the x-coordinate remains unchanged. Original point (3, 2) → reflected point (3, −2)."

Nate applied the reflection rule to determine where the artefact was hidden: original position (4, 3), reflected across the x-axis. "x stays the same — still 4. y changes sign: 3 becomes −3. Reflected position: (4, −3)." The artefact was recovered from that precise location.
🪞 Reflections

Across y-axis: x changes sign, y stays. (3, 2) → (−3, 2).
Across x-axis: y changes sign, x stays. (3, 2) → (3, −2).

Page 9 · The Coordinate Path
Eight artefacts had been recovered. Only one remained — the most precious of all: the Golden Compass of Numerica itself. Its location was given not as a single coordinate but as a coordinate path — a sequence of waypoints to navigate through in order.

"The path is: Start at (0, 0) → proceed to (3, 2) → then to (7, 2) → then to (7, 8) → final artefact at (5, 5)," Cora read from the intelligence dispatch. "We plot each waypoint and draw connecting segments in sequence."

"Importantly, we can calculate the total distance of the path by finding the length of each segment. From (0, 0) to (3, 2): we move 3 right and 2 up. From (3, 2) to (7, 2): we move 4 right and 0 up — a horizontal distance of 4. From (7, 2) to (7, 8): 0 right and 6 up — a vertical distance of 6. From (7, 8) to (5, 5): we move 2 left and 3 down."

Nate drew the complete path on the chart. Each segment was drawn with a ruler, precise and deliberate. The route formed a distinctive shape across the grid — mathematical navigation at its most elegant.

Following the path exactly, the two navigators arrived at (5, 5) and discovered the Golden Compass, gleaming beneath a stone slab.
🗺️ Coordinate Paths

Following a coordinate path: plot each point and connect in order. The path tells the story of the journey across the grid. Distance between two points on a horizontal or vertical line = difference in that coordinate.

Page 10 · Mission Complete
Captain Cora and Navigator Nate stood in the Great Hall of the Maritime Academy, surrounded by all nine recovered artefacts — each one carefully labelled with the coordinate at which it had been found. The Academy's Director regarded them with immense pride.

"You have demonstrated not only bravery but mathematical precision," the Director declared. "Every artefact was located using nothing more than a grid, two axes, and the elegant language of coordinates. You read coordinates, plotted them, identified shapes, calculated midpoints, applied translations and reflections, and followed complex paths — all with complete accuracy."

Cora stepped forward. "The coordinate system is one of mathematics' greatest inventions. Developed over centuries, it allows us to describe any point in space with absolute precision using only two numbers. It is the foundation of navigation, cartography, engineering, computer graphics, satellite positioning, and space exploration."

"Every point on every map in the world," Nate added, "is described by exactly this principle — an x-value and a y-value, always written in that order." The Golden Compass of Numerica was returned to its place of honour in the Academy's collection, and the coordinate mission was complete.
Coordinates let you describe ANY point in space precisely. Used in maps, GPS, computer graphics, and space navigation — this is mathematics that changes the world!
