Page 2 · Case 1 — Tally Charts
The Data Detective Academy was a special school where young mathematicians learned to use data to solve real mysteries. Director Priya stood at the front of the classroom with a stern expression and said, "Welcome, cadets. Data is the key to every mystery. Your first case begins now."

Cadet Ollie leaned forward eagerly. The first case involved the city museum, which had been struggling with visitor numbers. Someone had collected data in a tally chart — one of the simplest ways to record information quickly.

Director Priya explained: "In a tally chart, we make a mark for each item we count. After four marks, the fifth mark is drawn across them — like a gate — to make a group of five. This makes counting quick and easy."

Ollie studied the chart carefully. Monday showed four marks. Tuesday showed five marks and two more — a group of five, then two. That must be seven visitors on Tuesday.
📋 Tally Charts

TALLY CHARTS group data in fives:
|||| = 4, |||| | = 5, |||| || = 7
Count tallies to find totals quickly — the fifth mark crosses the first four.

🤔 Think About It!

A tally chart shows: Mon ||||, Tue |||| ||. How many visitors on Tuesday?

Page 3
Suspect D had the most sightings — bar chart reveals the clue
Page 3 · Case 2 — Bar Charts
Case 2 was more urgent. The Academy had received a report about a mysterious figure seen near the museum at different times throughout the week. Five suspects — labelled A through E — had all been spotted, but which one had been seen the most?

Director Priya introduced the second tool in a detective's data kit: the bar chart. "A bar chart uses bars of different heights to represent different values," she explained. "The taller the bar, the larger the value. Always read the scale on the vertical axis carefully."

Ollie studied the chart. Suspect D had the tallest bar, reaching up to 9. That meant suspect D had been seen nine times — far more than any of the others. This was an important clue.

"The bar chart makes comparison instant," Ollie said. "In a long list of numbers, you might miss things. A bar chart shows differences clearly at a glance."
📊 Bar Charts

BAR CHARTS show data with bars.
The HEIGHT of each bar shows the value.
Read the scale on the vertical axis carefully to get accurate values.

🤔 Think About It!

A bar chart shows temperatures: Mon 12°, Tue 15°, Wed 10°. Which day was warmest?

Page 4
Temperature rises to a peak on Wednesday — line graph reveals the trend
Page 4 · Case 3 — Line Graphs Introduction
Case 3 required a new kind of chart entirely. The Academy had been tracking the temperature outside the museum over six days, and the data needed to show how the temperature changed from one day to the next — not just what it was on each individual day.

"For data that changes over time," Director Priya said, tapping the whiteboard, "we use a LINE GRAPH. We plot each data point, then connect the points in order with a line. The shape of the line tells us the story."

Ollie studied the graph. From Monday to Wednesday, the line rose steeply — it was getting warmer. Then it began to fall. "A rising line means an increase," Ollie said. "A falling line means a decrease."

"Exactly," said Director Priya. "A line graph is the best tool for showing change over time."
📈 Line Graphs

A LINE GRAPH connects data points with a line.
It shows how data CHANGES OVER TIME.
Rising line = increasing. Falling line = decreasing.

🤔 Think About It!

On the graph above, between which two days did temperature increase the most?

Page 5
Reading values from the rainfall line graph — April vs May
Page 5 · Reading Line Graphs — Finding Values
The Academy's next clue involved rainfall data. A line graph showed how much rain fell each month from January to June. Ollie needed to read specific values from the graph to compare two months.

Director Priya showed him the correct technique: "Find the month you want on the horizontal axis. Then move straight upward until you reach the line. From that point, move horizontally across to the vertical axis and read the value."

Ollie located April on the graph and read across to the vertical axis: 35 mm of rain. Then he found May: 25 mm. He could now compare the two months.

"April had 10 mm more rain than May," Ollie said. "The line graph makes this easy to read — no tables of numbers required, just a steady eye and a ruler."
🔍 Reading Line Graph Values

Find the month on the x-axis, go straight up to the line, then read across to the y-axis.
April = 35 mm, May = 25 mm.
Difference = 35 − 25 = 10 mm

🤔 Think About It!

April had 35 mm of rain. May had 25 mm. How much less rain in May?

Ollie plots point (4, 30) — find x=4 then up to y=30
Page 6 · Plotting Points on a Line Graph
Now it was Ollie's turn to create a graph, not just read one. Director Priya handed Ollie a table of crime report data for six months and said, "Plot each piece of data as a point on the grid, then connect the points in order."

Ollie began carefully. To plot a point with coordinates (4, 30), he first found the number 4 on the horizontal axis. Then he located 30 on the vertical axis. He drew a light pencil line up from 4 and across from 30. Where the two lines crossed was exactly where the point should go.

He placed a dot and continued with each data point, one by one. Once all five were plotted, he connected them with a smooth line. The result was a line graph that clearly showed how crime reports had changed over the months.

"Plotting takes patience," Ollie said, "but each point has to be in exactly the right place, or the graph will mislead everyone."
✏️ Plotting Points

To PLOT a point (x, y):
Find x on the horizontal axis, find y on the vertical axis, put a dot where they meet.
Connect dots in order with a line.

🤔 Think About It!

You want to plot the point (4, 30) on a graph. Where is it?

Page 7
Crime reports follow a clear downward trend over 8 months
Page 7 · Trend Analysis — Is Crime Increasing or Decreasing?
The crime data Ollie had plotted revealed something important. Looking at the line graph over eight months, it was clear that the number of crime reports at the museum had been falling steadily. Each month was lower than the last.

"This is called a TREND," Director Priya said. "A trend is the overall direction of change shown by a line graph. If the line goes up over time, we have an upward trend — things are increasing. If it falls, we have a downward trend."

"We can even draw a trend line," she added, drawing a straight dashed line through the middle of the plotted points. "This helps us see the pattern clearly, even if individual points jump up or down."

Ollie nodded. "And if we can see the trend, we can make a prediction about what might happen next month — even before the data is collected!"
📉 Trend Analysis

A TREND is the overall direction of change.
Rising line = upward trend (increasing).
Falling line = downward trend (decreasing).
Trends help us make predictions.

🤔 Think About It!

Sales rise from 100 to 120 to 140 to 160 over 4 months. What might sales be next month?

Page 8
Same data — two very different looking graphs! Always check the y-axis
Page 8 · Misleading Graphs — Not All Graphs Tell the Truth!
Ollie was shocked by what Director Priya showed him next: two graphs showing exactly the same data, but looking completely different. One showed a tiny difference between two months. The other made the same difference look enormous.

"How is that possible?" Ollie asked, leaning in to look more closely.

Director Priya pointed to the vertical axis on each graph. "The first graph starts at zero — as all well-made graphs should. The second graph starts at 90. Because the vertical axis only shows a tiny range, even a small change looks like a dramatic leap."

"This is called a misleading graph," Priya said firmly. "People sometimes use them to make small changes look huge, or to make big changes look small. A good data detective always checks the scale."

Ollie nodded gravely. From now on, he would always read the axis scale before drawing any conclusions from a graph.
⚠️ Misleading Graphs

A y-axis that does not start at 0 can make small changes look huge.
Always check the scale before interpreting a graph.
A good detective never assumes — they verify the axes first.

🤔 Think About It!

Two graphs show the same data. One y-axis starts at 0, one starts at 90. Which looks more dramatic?

Director Priya and Ollie present the evidence — case solved!
Page 9 · The Case Is Solved!
The Academy's largest conference room was packed. Detectives from across the city had gathered to hear Director Priya and cadet Ollie present their findings. A large board displayed all the evidence — tally charts, bar charts, line graphs, and trend analyses.

"The data tells a clear story," Director Priya began. "Suspect D was seen most frequently, as confirmed by the bar chart. The museum's visitor numbers have been falling — shown by the line graph trend. And the culprit was able to enter during the quietest period, identified by the tally chart."

Ollie stepped forward proudly to add: "We also spotted a misleading graph in the suspect's files, designed to hide a large change. But we checked the axis and saw through it immediately."

The room burst into applause. Data and graphs had revealed the truth — every piece of evidence had played its part.
🔍 Choosing the Right Chart

Tally charts: collect and count data quickly.
Bar charts: compare groups or categories.
Line graphs: show how data changes over time.

Graduation Day — Ollie earns the Data Detective badge!
Page 10 · Graduation Day!
On graduation day, the Academy's hall was decorated with graphs of all kinds. Charts lined the walls, each one telling a different story about the world. Cadet Ollie stood in the front row, wearing a crisp new detective badge, as Director Priya called the cadets forward one by one.

"A qualified data detective," Priya said in her calm, clear voice, "is someone who can collect data carefully, choose the right type of chart for the job, read values accurately, identify trends with confidence, and spot when a graph has been made misleading."

Ollie thought back over the cases: the museum tally chart, the suspect bar chart, the rainfall line graph, the crime trend, the misleading axes. Every lesson had built on the last.

"Data is everywhere," Director Priya told the graduating class. "In science, in sport, in business, in everyday life. When you understand how to read and create graphs, you can understand the world around you — and that is a powerful thing indeed."
📝 What You Have Learned

A good data detective can:
Read tally charts, interpret bar charts,
plot and analyse line graphs,
spot trends and identify misleading displays.
