Page 2 · What Is Symmetry?
Sam the Symmetry Detective was the best detective in all of Shapeworld — a magical land where shapes came alive.

One morning, Sam received a message: "Symmetry is missing from our shapes! Please help!"

Her partner Kit handed her the magnifying glass. "Where do we start?"

Sam picked up a large mirror. "First, Kit — we must understand what symmetry means."

She held the mirror next to a butterfly. "A shape has symmetry if you can fold it in half and both sides match perfectly."

"The fold line is called the line of symmetry," she explained. "The mirror shows us this line!"

Kit looked at a lopsided blob. "That one's not symmetric — the two halves are different shapes!"
Symmetry means both halves match perfectly! Fold it along the line of symmetry and the two halves line up exactly — like a mirror reflection!
🪞 Maths Fact: Line of Symmetry

A shape has symmetry if you can fold it exactly in half and both halves match. The fold line is the LINE OF SYMMETRY.

Page 3 · Squares and Rectangles
The first shape in Shapeworld that had lost its symmetry was a square.

Sam drew all the lines of symmetry carefully. "A square is very special," she said.

"It has four lines of symmetry — a horizontal one, a vertical one, and two diagonals!"

Kit picked up a rectangle and tried the same. "The diagonal doesn't work!" he cried.

"Correct," said Sam. "A rectangle only has 2 lines — horizontal and vertical. The diagonal cuts it into two different-shaped triangles, so they don't match."

Kit looked impressed. "So a square has more symmetry than a rectangle, even though they both have four right angles?"

"Exactly," said Sam. "Because a square has all four sides equal, the diagonals work as symmetry lines too."
A square has 4 lines of symmetry: horizontal, vertical, and 2 diagonals. A rectangle only has 2 lines — the diagonals do not work because the sides are different lengths!
⬛ Maths Fact: Square vs Rectangle

A square has 4 lines of symmetry (2 straight + 2 diagonal). A rectangle has only 2 lines (horizontal and vertical — NOT the diagonals).

Page 4 · Equilateral Triangles
Next, Sam and Kit found a triangle in distress. It was an equilateral triangle — one with all three sides exactly equal.

"All three sides are the same length," Sam observed. "And all three angles are the same size too — 60 degrees each."

Kit drew the lines of symmetry carefully. "One from the top corner down to the bottom... one from the bottom-left corner across... one from the bottom-right corner across..."

"Three lines!" Kit exclaimed. "One from each corner, going to the midpoint of the opposite side!"

Sam nodded. "That is why equilateral triangles are beautiful — all three sides equal means all three lines work."

"But a scalene triangle, where all sides are different, has zero lines of symmetry," Sam added.

Kit wrote it in the notebook: equilateral triangle = 3 lines of symmetry.
An equilateral triangle has 3 lines of symmetry — one from each corner to the midpoint of the opposite side. All sides equal means all lines work!
🔺 Maths Fact: Equilateral Triangle

An equilateral triangle (all sides equal) has exactly 3 lines of symmetry — one from each corner to the opposite side's midpoint.

Page 5 · Circles — Infinite Symmetry!
Sam and Kit arrived at the circle district of Shapeworld. The circle was spinning happily.

"How many lines of symmetry does a circle have?" Kit asked, ready with his pencil.

Sam smiled. "That is the best thing about circles, Kit. Draw a line through the centre — any line at all — and you get a line of symmetry!"

Kit tried. He drew a horizontal line — both halves matched. Then a diagonal — still matched! Then another and another...

"I could draw lines all day and they would all work!" Kit cried.

"Exactly," said Sam. "A circle has infinite lines of symmetry. That word means unlimited — more than any number you can name!"

"Circles are the most symmetric shapes of all," Sam concluded. "That is why wheels are round!"
A circle has INFINITE lines of symmetry! Any straight line drawn through the centre of a circle is a line of symmetry. You can draw as many as you like!
⭕ Maths Fact: Circle = Infinite Symmetry

A circle has INFINITE (∞) lines of symmetry — any straight line through the centre is a line of symmetry!

Page 6 · Letters and Numbers
Sam and Kit walked into the Alphabet District of Shapeworld. Letters were everywhere — on signs, lampposts, and banners.

"Symmetry hides in letters too!" Sam announced, pulling out her mirror.

She held the mirror next to the letter A. "Look — the left side mirrors the right side. A vertical line of symmetry!"

Kit tried the letter B. "The top mirrors the bottom — a horizontal line!"

Sam showed him H. "H has both — a vertical AND a horizontal line of symmetry."

Then Kit tried S. "S doesn't work," he said sadly. "Rotating it gives the same shape, but folding does not."

"Correct," said Sam. "S has rotational symmetry but no line of symmetry. That is a different kind of symmetry!"
A has a vertical line of symmetry. B has a horizontal line. H has both! But S and Z have no lines of symmetry at all. Look for letters in your own name!
🔤 Maths Fact: Letter Symmetry

Many letters have symmetry. A has a vertical line. B has a horizontal line. H has both! S, Z, F, G, J — have no lines of symmetry.

Page 7 · Completing Half a Shape
The hardest challenge yet! A butterfly had lost its right wing entirely.

"Only the left wing remains," Sam said gravely. "We must recreate the right wing exactly."

"How?" asked Kit, staring at the empty space on the right side.

"We use the line of symmetry," Sam explained. "Every point on the left wing has a matching point on the right wing, at the same distance from the mirror line."

Sam placed tracing paper over the left wing, folded along the mirror line, and pressed. The right wing shape appeared perfectly.

"That process is called reflecting," Sam said. "When you reflect a shape across the mirror line, you get the matching half."

Kit drew the right wing carefully. It matched perfectly. The butterfly was whole again!
To complete a symmetric shape, reflect one half across the mirror line! Each point on one side has a matching point on the other side at the same distance from the line.
↔️ Maths Skill: Reflecting Shapes

If you know one half of a symmetric shape, reflect it across the mirror line to find the other half. Each point matches at the same distance from the line.

Page 8 · Real-World Symmetry
As Sam and Kit walked through Shapeworld, they began to see symmetry everywhere around them.

"Look at that butterfly!" cried Kit. "One vertical line of symmetry — left wing mirrors right wing."

Sam pointed at a five-pointed star. "A star has five lines of symmetry — one from each point to the opposite gap!"

They saw a flower with petals arranged equally all the way around. "Many lines of symmetry — just like a circle!" Sam said.

Even the aeroplanes flying overhead had symmetry — the left wing was a perfect mirror of the right wing.

"Symmetry makes things balanced and beautiful," Sam said thoughtfully. "Nature and designers both use it."

Kit looked around with new eyes. Symmetry really was hiding everywhere!
Symmetry appears in nature and design everywhere! Buildings, leaves, animals, logos — once you know what to look for, you will see it all around you!
🌍 Symmetry in the Real World

Symmetry appears in nature (butterflies, flowers, leaves) and design (buildings, logos, planes). It creates balance and beauty!

Page 9 · Mission Accomplished!
Sam and Kit stood back and looked at Shapeworld. Every shape was restored. Every line of symmetry was back in place.

The square shone with its four lines. The equilateral triangle gleamed with its three. The circle glowed with its infinite lines.

Even the letters on the signs were properly symmetric again — H, A, M, O all perfectly balanced.

"We did it!" Kit cheered, throwing his arms in the air. "Every shape has its symmetry back!"

Sam smiled. "And we restored them all using just one tool — the knowledge of lines of symmetry."

The shapes of Shapeworld applauded. The circle spun with joy. The triangle did a little hop.

"Remember," Sam told Kit as they packed away their detective tools, "symmetry is not just for shapes — it is everywhere you look."
📋 Lines of Symmetry — Summary!

✅ Circle — ∞ (infinite) lines
✅ Square — 4 lines (H, V, 2 diagonals)
✅ Equilateral triangle — 3 lines
✅ Rectangle — 2 lines (H and V only)
✅ Most irregular shapes — 0 lines
