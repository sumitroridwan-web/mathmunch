Pip's Pizza Palace — open for business!
Page 2 · The Story Begins
Pip the Fox had the best pizza shop in Mathville.

Every morning, Pip baked fresh pizzas with golden crusts, bubbling cheese, and colourful toppings.

One sunny day, Pip's best friend Leo the Lion came running into the shop.
Pip! I'm so hungry and your pizzas smell amazing. Can I have one?
Pip smiled warmly. "Of course, Leo! But I only have one pizza left — and we must share it fairly."

Leo thought for a moment. "What does fairly mean?"
💡 Maths Word: Fair Share

A fair share means each person gets exactly the same amount. When we cut something into equal pieces, each piece is a fraction of the whole.

Page 3 · Halves
Pip picked up a pizza cutter and sliced straight down the middle.

"There!" said Pip. "Now there are TWO equal pieces."

"Each piece is called ONE HALF," Pip explained. "We write it like this: ½"
✂️ Fractions: Halves

When we cut something into 2 equal pieces, each piece is one half.

12
= one half = 50% of the whole

The bottom number (2) tells us how many equal pieces in total.
The top number (1) tells us how many pieces we are talking about.

🤔 Think About It!

Pip and Leo each get ½ of the pizza. They eat both pieces. How much pizza is left?

4 equal pieces — one for everyone!
Page 4 · Quarters
Just as Leo and Pip were about to eat, Bea the Bear and Milo the Mouse came through the door!

"Oh no," said Leo. "Now there are FOUR of us and only one pizza!"

Pip winked. "No problem! We cut it into FOUR equal pieces — called quarters!"
Each of us gets one quarter. We write that as ¼. It is fair for everyone!
🔪 Fractions: Quarters

When we cut something into 4 equal pieces, each piece is one quarter.

14
= one quarter = 25% of the whole
44
= four quarters = 1 whole pizza
🤔 Think About It!

Leo eats 2 quarters (he is very hungry!). How much pizza did Leo eat?

3 friends, 3 equal slices — thirds!
Page 5 · Thirds
The next day, Pip baked a fresh pizza. This time, only three friends came — Pip, Leo, and Bea.

"How do we share it between THREE people?" asked Bea.

Pip drew three lines from the centre, each at the same angle. "We cut it into THREE equal pieces — called thirds!"
Each piece is one third of the whole pizza. We write it as ⅓. Three thirds make one whole!
✂️ Fractions: Thirds

When we cut something into 3 equal pieces, each piece is one third.

13
= one third ≈ 33% of the whole

Compare the sizes:
½ > ⅓ > ¼ — a half is bigger than a third, which is bigger than a quarter!

Page 6 · Comparing Fractions
Bea looked at the fraction bar chart on Pip's wall and scratched her head.

"Pip, which slice is bigger — a half, a third, or a quarter?"

Pip pointed to the chart. "Look — when you cut a pizza into MORE pieces, each piece gets SMALLER!"
A half is the biggest slice of these three. A quarter is the smallest — because we split the pizza into more pieces!
⚖️ Comparing Fractions (Same Whole)

When the whole is the same size, a fraction with a bigger bottom number gives you a smaller piece.

Page 8 · The End
That evening, all the friends sat together in Pip's cosy pizza shop.

They had learned that fractions are all about sharing equally — something friends do best!

Halves, thirds, quarters — no matter how many friends arrived, Pip always found a way to share fairly.
Fractions aren't just maths — they're how we share everything in life! Every time you split something equally, you're using fractions.
📝 What We Learned

✅ A fraction names equal parts of a whole
✅ ½ = 1 out of 2 equal parts (half)
✅ ⅓ = 1 out of 3 equal parts (third)
✅ ¼ = 1 out of 4 equal parts (quarter)
✅ More pieces = smaller each piece
✅ ½ > ⅓ > ¼ when the whole is the same
