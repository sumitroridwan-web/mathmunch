Page 2 · The Kingdom is Divided
Deep within the mathematical realm of Numerica, there existed a prosperous kingdom called the Fraction Kingdom. For generations, its citizens had lived in harmony, guided by the laws of arithmetic and proportion. However, on one fateful morning, a great dispute arose in the royal court that threatened to divide the kingdom in two.

Queen Fracta stood on the left side of the throne room, holding a banner displaying the fraction ½. Opposite her, King Whole raised his standard bearing the fraction 2/4. Each monarch declared their fraction to be distinct and superior — neither would yield to the other.

"½ and 2/4 are entirely different fractions!" declared Queen Fracta with absolute certainty. "The denominator is different, therefore the values must differ!"

King Whole shook his head vigorously. "They look different, but I believe they represent the same quantity. However, I cannot prove it without the aid of our Royal Mathematician!"

The court fell into uproar. Nobles argued on both sides, and no resolution could be found through debate alone. Only one person possessed the knowledge and analytical rigour to settle the matter definitively.
Send for Zeph, the Royal Mathematician! Only precise mathematical proof can settle this royal dispute and restore unity to the Fraction Kingdom.

Page 3 · Zeph's Method
Royal Mathematician Zeph arrived in the throne room carrying a large scroll filled with diagrams and equations. Zeph had spent years studying at the Academy of Numerical Sciences and was renowned throughout Numerica for producing elegant, rigorous mathematical proofs.

"Your Majesties," Zeph began, unrolling the scroll across the royal table, "the dispute concerns whether two fractions that appear visually different can nonetheless represent identical quantities. The answer lies in the concept of equivalent fractions."

Zeph drew a rectangle and divided it first into 3 equal parts, shading one section. "This represents one third — 1/3." Then Zeph drew an identical rectangle divided into 6 equal parts and shaded two sections. "This also represents one third — but written as 2/6."

"The shaded regions are identical in size," Zeph explained carefully, "because we multiplied both the numerator and the denominator by the same value — in this case, by 2. When you multiply or divide both parts of a fraction by the same non-zero number, the fraction's value is preserved exactly."

The court leaned forward, beginning to understand. Zeph wrote the complete chain: 1/3 = 2/6 = 3/9 = 4/12.

Page 4 · Simplifying Fractions
Having demonstrated that fractions can be expressed in multiple equivalent forms, Zeph moved on to the next critical skill: simplifying fractions, also known as reducing a fraction to its lowest terms.

"A fraction is considered fully simplified," Zeph explained to the assembled court, "when the numerator and denominator share no common factor other than 1. To achieve this, we divide both numbers by their Highest Common Factor, abbreviated as HCF."

Zeph selected the fraction 12/16 as a demonstration. "What is the HCF of 12 and 16? We list the factors of each: factors of 12 are 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12. Factors of 16 are 1, 2, 4, 8, 16. The highest number appearing in both lists is 4."

"Therefore: 12 ÷ 4 = 3 and 16 ÷ 4 = 4. The simplified fraction is 3/4. We verify that 3 and 4 share no common factor other than 1 — confirmed, so we are done."

Several nobles nodded with increasing comprehension. The method was systematic, reliable, and produced a unique simplest form for every fraction.
Worked Example: Simplify 18/24
1Factors of 18: 1, 2, 3, 6, 9, 18. Factors of 24: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 24.
2Highest Common Factor = 6
318 ÷ 6 = 3    24 ÷ 6 = 4
4Answer: 18/24 = 3/4 (simplest form, since HCF of 3 and 4 = 1)
✂️ Simplifying Fractions

To SIMPLIFY: divide numerator and denominator by their HCF. 12/16 ÷ 4/4 = 3/4. A fraction is in simplest form when HCF = 1.

Page 5 · Comparing Fractions
With the concept of equivalence now established, Zeph turned to a practical application that the court immediately recognised as relevant to royal life: comparing fractions to determine which is larger.

"Suppose we wish to compare 2/3 and 3/5," Zeph said, writing both fractions on the royal chalkboard. "We cannot simply compare the numerators or denominators in isolation, because the denominators are different — we are not comparing like with like."

"The solution is to find a Common Denominator — specifically, the Lowest Common Denominator (LCD), which is the smallest number that both denominators divide into evenly." Zeph calculated: "The LCD of 3 and 5 is 15, because 3 × 5 = 15 and 5 × 3 = 15."

"Convert each fraction: 2/3 = 10/15 (multiplying by 5/5), and 3/5 = 9/15 (multiplying by 3/3). Now we compare: 10/15 versus 9/15. Since 10 is greater than 9, we conclude that 2/3 > 3/5."

The court applauded. The method was entirely systematic and free from ambiguity.
⚖️ Comparing Fractions

To compare fractions: find a COMMON DENOMINATOR. 2/3 = 10/15; 3/5 = 9/15. So 2/3 > 3/5 because 10/15 > 9/15.

Page 6 · Adding Fractions — Same Denominator
The court's understanding was growing with every lesson from Zeph. Now the Royal Mathematician turned to the operations that would allow the kingdom's ministers to calculate with fractions: addition and subtraction.

"We begin with the simpler case," Zeph said methodically. "Adding fractions that share the same denominator — what we call like fractions. The rule is remarkably straightforward: add the numerators together, and keep the denominator unchanged."

"Consider the Royal Treasury's grain stores: the Eastern Granary holds 2/7 of the kingdom's grain, and the Western Granary holds 3/7. What fraction of the total supply do both granaries contain together?"

Zeph wrote clearly on the board: 2/7 + 3/7 = (2 + 3)/7 = 5/7. "We only add the top numbers — the numerators. The denominator 7 tells us the size of each portion, and that does not change when we combine portions of the same size."

"A common error is to add the denominators as well, producing 5/14 — this is incorrect. The denominator must remain 7 because each portion is still one-seventh in size," Zeph warned.

Page 7 · Adding Fractions — Different Denominators
The treasury ministers now presented Zeph with a more challenging scenario: they needed to add fractions with different denominators. The Royal Baker required 1/3 of the flour supplies and the Royal Chef needed 1/4 — what total fraction of the flour stores would be consumed?

"This is the most common challenge when working with fractions in practical situations," Zeph acknowledged. "We cannot simply add 1/3 and 1/4 directly, because the pieces are different sizes — thirds and quarters are not the same unit."

"The solution follows three mandatory steps. Step 1: Find the Lowest Common Denominator. Multiples of 3: 3, 6, 9, 12 ... Multiples of 4: 4, 8, 12 ... The LCD is 12."

"Step 2: Convert both fractions to twelfths. 1/3 = 4/12 (multiply by 4/4). 1/4 = 3/12 (multiply by 3/3). Now both fractions have the same denominator — we have like fractions."

"Step 3: Add the numerators. 4/12 + 3/12 = 7/12. Answer: 1/3 + 1/4 = 7/12."
🔢 Adding Different-Denominator Fractions

Step 1: Find LCD. Step 2: Convert both. Step 3: Add. 1/3 + 1/4 = 4/12 + 3/12 = 7/12.

Page 8 · Subtracting Fractions
The Royal Treasury now needed to calculate how much of the kingdom's resources remained after expenditure. This required subtracting fractions — and Zeph assured the court that the rules were entirely parallel to those for addition.

"For fractions with the same denominator, the process mirrors addition exactly: subtract the second numerator from the first, and retain the denominator. The Royal Library held 5/6 of its book budget. After purchasing volumes for the new wing, 1/6 was spent. Remaining budget: 5/6 − 1/6 = 4/6 = 2/3."

"Notice that I simplified 4/6 to 2/3 — dividing both by 2. Always simplify your final answer when possible."

"For fractions with different denominators, we again apply the three-step method: find the LCD, convert, then subtract. The Royal Stables used 3/4 of their feed allocation, but returned 1/3 to the stores. Net consumption: 3/4 − 1/3."

"LCD of 4 and 3 is 12. Convert: 3/4 = 9/12 and 1/3 = 4/12. Subtract: 9/12 − 4/12 = 5/12."
➖ Subtracting Fractions

Same denominator → subtract numerators. Different → find LCD first. 5/6 − 1/6 = 4/6 = 2/3.   3/4 − 1/3 = 9/12 − 4/12 = 5/12.

Page 9 · Mixed Numbers
The final mathematical concept Zeph needed to address before settling the royal dispute was the notion of mixed numbers — fractions that are greater than one whole.

"In practical life," Zeph explained, "we frequently encounter quantities larger than a single whole unit. For instance, the Royal Baker used one and three-quarter sacks of flour — written as 1¾. This is called a mixed number because it combines a whole number part with a fractional part."

"Mathematicians also express such quantities as improper fractions, where the numerator is greater than the denominator. Converting 1¾ to an improper fraction requires two steps."

"Multiply the whole number by the denominator: 1 × 4 = 4. Then add the numerator: 4 + 3 = 7. Place this result over the original denominator: 7/4. Therefore 1¾ = 7/4."

"Conversely, to convert an improper fraction back to a mixed number, divide the numerator by the denominator. 7 ÷ 4 = 1 remainder 3, giving us 1 and 3/4 — which is precisely 1¾. Both forms are mathematically equivalent."
Worked Example: Convert 2½ to an improper fraction
1Identify: whole = 2, numerator = 1, denominator = 2
2Multiply: 2 × 2 = 4
3Add numerator: 4 + 1 = 5
4Answer: 2½ = 5/2
🔄 Mixed Numbers

A MIXED NUMBER has a whole part and a fraction: 1¾. Convert to improper fraction: (1×4)+3 = 7, so 1¾ = 7/4.

Page 10 · The Kingdom Unites
The throne room fell completely silent as Zeph prepared to deliver the final verdict. Every noble, minister, and royal guard held their breath, waiting for the mathematical proof that would determine the kingdom's future.

"Your Majesties," Zeph announced with calm authority, "I am pleased to present the definitive proof. Using the principles of equivalent fractions that we have examined today, I can demonstrate conclusively that ½ and 2/4 are, in fact, precisely equal in value."

"Observe: 1/2 × 2/2 = 2/4. We have multiplied both numerator and denominator by 2 — a valid equivalence transformation — confirming that ½ = 2/4. Further, both equal 4/8, and both equal the decimal 0.5, and both represent 50% of any whole quantity."

"There is no dispute. Queen Fracta's ½ and King Whole's 2/4 are two names for the same mathematical truth."

Queen Fracta and King Whole looked at each other for a long moment — then both burst into warm laughter. The court erupted in celebration. The Fraction Kingdom was united, and its citizens would henceforth work together, guided by the elegant, unambiguous language of mathematics.
Mathematics does not take sides — it reveals truth. Equivalent fractions, simplifying, comparing, adding and subtracting, mixed numbers: you have now mastered the complete fraction toolkit!
