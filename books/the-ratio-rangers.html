Sasha and Tom study ratio in the forest lab
Page 2 · What Is Ratio?
Deep in the Emerald Forest, two young Rangers — Sasha and Tom — have been given a critical mission. The ancient potions that protect the forest from drought have run dry, and only the Rangers know how to brew them correctly. Every ingredient must be mixed in exact ratios, or the potion will fail.

Sasha opens the worn leather recipe book. The first line reads: "Mix green moss and blue crystal in ratio 2 : 3."

Tom tilts his head. "What does ratio actually mean?" he asks. Sasha smiles and pulls out a strip of bark to explain. A ratio compares two or more quantities that are measured in the same units. When we write 2 : 3, we mean "for every 2 parts of one thing, there are 3 parts of another." Neither number is a fraction of the other — they simply describe the relative sizes.

Ratios can be simplified just like fractions. If we have 6 : 9, we find the highest common factor (HCF) of 6 and 9, which is 3. Dividing both sides by 3 gives us 2 : 3 — exactly the same ratio expressed in its simplest form. The ratio bar above shows this clearly: the proportions look identical no matter which equivalent form we use.
💡 Simplifying Ratios

To simplify a ratio, divide both parts by their HCF.
Example: 6 : 9 → HCF = 3 → 6 ÷ 3 : 9 ÷ 3 = 2 : 3
Check: the HCF of 2 and 3 is 1, so 2 : 3 is fully simplified.

Page 3
Multiply or divide both sides by the same number
Page 3 · Equivalent Ratios
Armed with the concept of ratio, the Rangers now need to scale up their recipe for a larger batch of potion. Sasha explains to Tom that equivalent ratios work exactly like equivalent fractions — they represent the same relationship between quantities, just expressed with different numbers.

"Think of 2 : 3 as our base ratio," Sasha says. "If we multiply both parts by 2, we get 4 : 6. Multiply both by 3, and we get 6 : 9. The visual proportions — the bar lengths — always look the same, because the relative sizes haven't changed."

Tom notices the pattern immediately. "So scaling up a ratio is just like finding an equivalent fraction — but for two quantities at once!" Exactly right. This principle is what makes cooking, architecture, and map-making possible. To find an equivalent ratio, multiply or divide both terms by the same non-zero number. You can never add or subtract the same value to both sides — that would change the relationship entirely.

The Rangers check the ratio 3 : 5. To find the equivalent ratio where the first part is 12, they ask: "What was 3 multiplied by to reach 12?" The answer is 4. So they multiply the second part by 4 as well: 5 × 4 = 20. The equivalent ratio is 12 : 20.
🔗 Finding Equivalent Ratios

Rule: Multiply (or divide) both parts by the same number.
3 : 5 → first part must become 12 → 12 ÷ 3 = 4 → multiply second part: 5 × 4 = 20
Answer: 3 : 5 = 12 : 20

Page 4
35 divided in ratio 2 : 5 gives 10 and 25
Page 4 · Dividing a Quantity in a Ratio
The Rangers reach a storage cave containing 35 units of enchanted soil. The ancient map states that the two forest zones must receive soil in the ratio 2 : 5 — the northern zone needing less, the southern zone needing more to support larger trees.

Tom counts the units nervously. "How do we split 35 in ratio 2 : 5?" Sasha draws three steps in the dirt. First, she adds the ratio parts: 2 + 5 = 7 total parts. This means the 35 units must be divided into 7 equal groups. Second, she calculates the value of one part: 35 ÷ 7 = 5 units per part. Third, she multiplies each ratio number by that value: the northern zone receives 2 × 5 = 10 units, and the southern zone receives 5 × 5 = 25 units.

Tom verifies the answer by checking: 10 + 25 = 35 — perfect, the total is preserved. He also verifies the ratio: 10 : 25 simplifies to 2 : 5 (dividing both by 5). The distribution is correct. This method — find total parts, find one part, multiply — works for any quantity divided in any ratio, no matter how large the numbers become.
➗ Method: Divide a Quantity in a Ratio

Step 1: Add ratio parts → 2 + 5 = 7
Step 2: One part = total ÷ parts → 35 ÷ 7 = 5
Step 3: Multiply each ratio number → 2 × 5 = 10; 5 × 5 = 25
Check: 10 + 25 = 35 ✓ and 10 : 25 = 2 : 5 ✓

Page 5
Proportion tells you each part's share of the whole
Page 5 · Proportion
While measuring the soil, Tom asks a subtly different question: "Out of all the soil we have, what fraction goes to each zone?" This is the concept of proportion — and it is closely related to, but distinct from, ratio.

Sasha explains carefully. Ratio compares one part to another part. Proportion compares one part to the whole. If the ratio of green to blue is 2 : 3, then the total number of parts is 2 + 3 = 5. The proportion that is green is 2 out of 5, which we write as the fraction 2/5. The proportion that is blue is 3 out of 5, written as 3/5. And 2/5 + 3/5 = 1 — the whole — which confirms everything accounts for itself.

This distinction matters enormously in real life. A class of 30 students has boys and girls in ratio 2 : 3. The proportion that are boys is 2/5. To find the actual number of boys: 2/5 × 30 = 12 boys. Knowing the proportion lets us calculate exact numbers from any total — a skill the Rangers will use again and again throughout their mission in the Emerald Forest.
📊 Ratio vs Proportion

Ratio — part : part (e.g., boys : girls = 2 : 3)
Proportion — part out of whole (e.g., boys = 2/5 of the class)
To find actual number: multiply proportion by total → 2/5 × 30 = 12 boys

In a class of 30 students, the ratio of boys to girls is 2 : 3. How many boys are there?

Page 6
Doubling rangers → water doubles (direct proportion)
Page 6 · Direct Proportion
As the Rangers trek deeper into the forest, they need to calculate water supplies. The rule in the Ranger handbook states: "Every ranger requires 3 litres of water per day." Sasha recognises this as a situation of direct proportion.

In direct proportion, when one quantity increases, the other increases at the same rate. Doubling the number of rangers doubles the water needed. Halving the rangers halves the water. The graph of two quantities in direct proportion is always a straight line through the origin.

The key number in direct proportion is the constant of proportionality, often written as k. Here, k = 3 (litres per ranger). The formula is: Water = 3 × Rangers, or W = 3n. Using this, for 7 rangers: W = 3 × 7 = 21 litres. The proportion table confirms every row has the same ratio 1 : 3, which proves direct proportion holds throughout.

Tom checks: "If we have 12 litres, how many rangers can we supply?" He divides: 12 ÷ 3 = 4 rangers. Direct proportion works in both directions — you can always divide to find k and then use it to solve for either quantity.
📈 Direct Proportion Formula

y is directly proportional to x: y = kx
k = constant of proportionality = y ÷ x (always the same)
4 rangers need 12L → k = 12 ÷ 4 = 3 → 7 rangers: 3 × 7 = 21L

🤔 Think About It!

If 4 rangers need 12 litres of water, how much water do 7 rangers need?

Page 7
Scale every ingredient by the same multiplier
Page 7 · Recipe Scaling
The Rangers have found the potion recipe in the cave, but it is written for only 4 portions. With 10 forest animals to protect, they must scale the recipe up. Every ingredient must change in the same proportion — if one ingredient increases by more or less than the others, the potion will be dangerously unbalanced.

Sasha calculates the scaling factor first. She divides the new amount by the old amount: 10 ÷ 4 = 2.5. This is the multiplier. Every ingredient gets multiplied by 2.5. The recipe calls for 200 ml of Moonberry juice → 200 × 2.5 = 500 ml. It calls for 3 scoops of Star powder → 3 × 2.5 = 7.5 scoops. Forest oil: 150 × 2.5 = 375 ml. Crystal shards: 2 × 2.5 = 5 units.

Tom notices that 7.5 scoops is an awkward amount. Sasha explains they can also scale using the unitary method: find the amount for 1 portion first (200 ÷ 4 = 50 ml), then multiply by 10 (50 × 10 = 500 ml). Both methods always give the same answer because they are both expressions of direct proportion. The key principle: every ingredient scales by the same factor so the ratio between all ingredients remains unchanged.
🧪 Two Methods for Scaling

Method 1 (multiplier): new ÷ old = multiplier → multiply all ingredients
Method 2 (unitary): amount ÷ old portions = 1-portion amount → × new portions
Both give the same result — choose whichever is easier for the numbers given.

🤔 Think About It!

A recipe for 4 portions uses 200g flour, 3 eggs, 150ml milk. Scale to 6 portions. How much flour is needed?

Page 8
Map scales use ratio to represent real distances
Page 8 · Map Scales
The Rangers unfold their map of the Emerald Forest. In the corner they see the notation 1 : 50 000. Tom stares at it. "What does this mean in practice?" Sasha explains that a map scale is simply a ratio between map distance and real distance.

The scale 1 : 50 000 means that 1 unit on the map represents 50 000 of the same units in reality. So 1 centimetre on the map corresponds to 50 000 centimetres — which equals 500 metres or 0.5 kilometres in the real world. When Sasha measures the path from the cave to the waterfall as 4 cm on the map, the real distance is 4 × 50 000 = 200 000 cm = 2 kilometres.

To convert real distances back to map distances, you divide. If the real distance between two ancient oaks is 1.5 km = 150 000 cm, then the map distance is 150 000 ÷ 50 000 = 3 cm. Tom practices with a new map at scale 1 : 25 000. On this map, 6 cm represents 6 × 25 000 = 150 000 cm = 1.5 km in reality. Map scales are one of the most practical everyday uses of ratio.
🗺️ Working With Map Scales

Real distance = map distance × scale factor
Map distance = real distance ÷ scale factor
Scale 1 : 25 000 → 1 cm = 25 000 cm = 250 m = 0.25 km
6 cm on map → 6 × 25 000 = 150 000 cm = 1.5 km

Page 9
Exchange rates are direct proportion relationships
Page 9 · Currency & Unit Conversions
After sealing the potions, the Rangers discover a locked chest. Inside is a scroll explaining that rare forest crystals must be purchased from a distant merchant — and the prices are listed in US Dollars (USD), but the Rangers only have Indonesian Rupiah (IDR). Sasha consults the exchange chart: 1 USD = 15 500 IDR.

Currency exchange is another direct proportion relationship. The exchange rate is the constant of proportionality. To convert 250 USD to IDR, Sasha multiplies: 250 × 15 500 = 3 875 000 IDR. Tom records this carefully. To convert 310 000 IDR back to USD, he divides: 310 000 ÷ 15 500 = 20 USD.

The same proportion method applies to unit conversions. If 1 inch = 2.54 cm, then 12 inches = 12 × 2.54 = 30.48 cm. If a distance is 75 cm, how many inches? 75 ÷ 2.54 ≈ 29.5 inches. In every case, you identify the conversion factor (the constant of proportionality), then multiply to go one direction and divide to go the other. The relationship is always linear and passes through zero, which is the hallmark of direct proportion.
💱 Currency Conversion as Proportion

Exchange rate: 1 USD = 15 500 IDR
USD → IDR: multiply by 15 500 → 250 × 15 500 = 3 875 000 IDR
IDR → USD: divide by 15 500 → 310 000 ÷ 15 500 = 20 USD

Using the rate 1 USD = 15 500 IDR, convert 310 000 IDR to USD.

Page 10
Three-way ratio shares preserve the total
Page 10 · Three-Way Ratio Sharing
The Rangers have saved the Emerald Forest — and as a reward, the Forest Council awards a prize of £360 to be shared among three Rangers in the ratio 1 : 2 : 3. The Ranger who led the planning receives the smallest share, the one who gathered ingredients receives the middle share, and the one who brewed the potions receives the largest.

Sasha applies the same method as before, extended to three parts. Step 1: add all ratio parts together: 1 + 2 + 3 = 6 total parts. Step 2: find the value of one part: £360 ÷ 6 = £60 per part. Step 3: multiply each person's ratio number by £60. Person A: 1 × £60 = £60. Person B: 2 × £60 = £120. Person C: 3 × £60 = £180.

Always verify the answer: £60 + £120 + £180 = £360. The total is preserved. Also check the ratio: 60 : 120 : 180 — dividing all by 60 gives 1 : 2 : 3. The method extends identically to four or more parts — simply add all ratio numbers to find the total parts, then proceed as before.
🏆 Three-Part Ratio Method

Step 1: Total parts = 1 + 2 + 3 = 6
Step 2: One part = £360 ÷ 6 = £60
Step 3: Shares = £60, £120, £180
Check: 60 + 120 + 180 = £360 ✓

Page 11
Combine ratio division with direct proportion
Page 11 · Mixed Ratio and Proportion Challenge
The final test before receiving the Ratio Ranger Badge requires solving a complex, multi-step problem using every skill learned throughout the mission. The Forest Council presents the following scenario:

"The forest has 240 enchanted crystals in total. They must be divided between the northern and southern labs in the ratio 3 : 5. The northern lab's share will then be used to produce protective shields — each shield requires exactly 1 crystal per batch, and the lab needs to produce 4 complete batches. Is the northern lab's share sufficient? If not, how many extra crystals are needed?"

Sasha works methodically. Step 1: Total parts = 3 + 5 = 8. Step 2: One part = 240 ÷ 8 = 30. Step 3: Northern lab receives 3 × 30 = 90 crystals. Step 4: The lab needs crystals for 4 batches. If 1 batch requires 90 crystals, then 4 batches require 90 × 4 = 360 crystals. Since the lab only has 90 but needs 360, it needs 270 more crystals.

Tom checks: maybe the question means each batch requires 1 crystal per shield, not 90. Context matters in multi-step problems. Always re-read the question and define your variables clearly before proceeding. This disciplined, step-by-step approach is what makes a Ratio Ranger.
🧩 Multi-Step Strategy

1. Identify all given information and what is asked
2. Apply ratio division to find each share
3. Apply direct proportion to scale as required
4. Always check your answer makes sense in context

🤔 Think About It!

A prize of £500 is split in ratio 2 : 3. The larger share is then converted at 1 USD = £0.80. How much is the larger share in USD?

📋 Complete Rules Summary

Simplify: a : b → divide both by HCF
Equivalent: multiply or divide both parts by the same number
Divide quantity: total parts → one part → multiply each ratio term
Proportion: part ÷ total → fraction of whole
Direct proportion: y = kx where k is constant
Map scales: real = map × scale factor
Currency: multiply or divide by exchange rate
