Page 2 · The Garden Plan — What Is Area?
Architect Aisha and Builder Ben had been given an exciting project: designing a new school garden for Mr. Park, the school's head teacher.

Mr. Park wanted the garden to have a vegetable patch, a flower bed, a small pond, and a winding path. He spread out the plan on a big table, and both Aisha and Ben leaned in to look carefully.

The plan showed a large grid, where each small square represented exactly one square metre of ground. Aisha pointed to the grid and said, "To work out how much space each section takes up, we need to calculate its AREA."

"Area tells us the amount of flat space inside any shape," Aisha explained to Ben. "We measure area in square units — square centimetres (cm²) or, for a garden like this, square metres (m²)."
📐 Maths Word: Area

AREA is the amount of space inside a shape.
We measure it in square units (cm², m²).
Count the squares — or use a formula!

🤔 Think About It!

A grid has 4 rows and 3 columns of squares. How many squares in total?

Page 3
24 squares counted — the vegetable patch
Page 3 · The Vegetable Patch
The first section of the garden was the vegetable patch — a large rectangular area where Mr. Park wanted to grow tomatoes, carrots, and lettuce.

Aisha measured it carefully: 6 metres long and 4 metres wide. She drew it on the grid plan, and both she and Ben counted the squares — there were twenty-four in total.

"Counting every square works," Ben said, "but it would take forever for a really big garden!"

Aisha smiled. "That is exactly why we have a formula. For any rectangle, Area = Length × Width. Six times four equals twenty-four square metres — done in one step."

Ben wrote 24 m² on the garden plan with a green marker. The vegetable patch was officially measured.
📐 Rectangle Area Formula

RECTANGLE AREA = Length × Width
A 6 m × 4 m vegetable patch has area = 6 × 4 = 24 m².
The square unit (m²) tells us area is measured in squares.

🤔 Think About It!

A rectangle is 5 m long and 3 m wide. What is its area?

Page 4
The flower bed: 8×2 = 16 m²
Page 4 · The Flower Bed — Area Equivalence
The flower bed was the most colourful section of the garden plan. Mr. Park wanted it packed with roses, daisies, and tulips. Aisha had designed it as a long, narrow rectangle: 8 metres long and 2 metres wide.

"Area equals eight times two, which is sixteen square metres," Ben said confidently, writing it down.

"Good!" said Aisha. "Now here is something interesting. What if the flower bed were a square instead — four metres by four metres? What would the area be then?"

Ben calculated quickly: 4 × 4 = 16 m². Exactly the same! He looked surprised. "Both shapes have an area of 16 m², but they look completely different. One is long and thin, the other is a square."

Aisha nodded. "That is called area equivalence — different shapes can have equal areas. The shape changes, but the amount of space inside stays the same."
🔁 Area Equivalence

Area = 8 × 2 = 16 m². Notice: 8 × 2 = 4 × 4 = 16.
Both rectangles have the same area but different shapes!
This is called area equivalence.

🤔 Think About It!

Two rectangles: 8 × 2 and 4 × 4. Which has the bigger area?

Page 5
Find the missing width of the pond
Page 5 · The Pond — Finding a Missing Dimension
Mr. Park had always wanted a small pond in the school garden. He told Aisha and Ben that it must have an area of exactly 20 m² to fit the number of plants he had chosen for around the edge.

"I also know that the pond must be 5 metres long," Mr. Park said, "because of where the paths run. But I don't know how wide to make it."

Ben scratched his head. "We know the area and we know one side. How do we find the missing side?"

Aisha reached for her pencil. "If Area = Length × Width, then Width = Area ÷ Length. We just rearrange the formula!" She wrote: Width = 20 ÷ 5 = 4 metres.

Ben double-checked: 5 × 4 = 20. That is correct! Mr. Park beamed with pleasure, and Aisha drew the pond on the plan — 5 metres long and 4 metres wide, with a neat blue border.
🔀 Finding a Missing Dimension

If you know AREA and ONE SIDE, divide to find the other.
Area = L × W, so W = Area ÷ L.
20 ÷ 5 = 4 m

🤔 Think About It!

A pond has area 20 m². It is 5 m long. How wide is it?

Page 6
Perimeter = the path all the way around
Page 6 · Perimeter — The Garden Fence
With all the garden sections planned, Ben turned to a new problem: the fence. Mr. Park needed a fence all the way around the outside of the garden to keep it safe.

"To order the right amount of fencing," Ben said, "I need to know the total distance around the garden, not the space inside it."

"That is the PERIMETER," Aisha said. "The perimeter is the total distance around the outside of a shape. For a rectangle, we add all four sides together."

She drew the garden rectangle on the planning board: 7 metres long and 3 metres wide. She added arrows to show the path going all the way around: 7 + 3 + 7 + 3 = 20 metres.

"Or," she added, "we can use the formula: P = 2 × (L + W) = 2 × (7 + 3) = 2 × 10 = 20 m." Ben ordered exactly 20 metres of fencing.
📏 Perimeter Formula

PERIMETER = the total distance AROUND a shape.
For a rectangle: P = 2 × (L + W)
P = 2 × (7 + 3) = 2 × 10 = 20 m

🤔 Think About It!

A rectangle is 9 m × 4 m. What is its perimeter?

Page 7
L-shaped path split into 2 rectangles
Page 7 · Composite Shapes — The L-Shaped Path
The garden path wound through the garden in an L-shape — a long horizontal section along the top, then a shorter section dropping down on one side. It looked beautiful on the plan, but calculating its area was trickier than a simple rectangle.

"How do we find the area of an L-shape?" Ben asked, turning the plan sideways and squinting at it.

"We split it into rectangles!" Aisha said brightly. She drew a line across the L-shape, dividing it into two separate rectangles. "Rectangle A is 6 metres by 2 metres. Rectangle B is 4 metres by 3 metres."

Ben calculated both: 6 × 2 = 12 m² and 4 × 3 = 12 m². Then he added them: 12 + 12 = 24 m².

"So the rule is: split any complicated shape into rectangles, find each area, then add them up," Ben said. Aisha gave a thumbs-up.
🔀 Composite Shapes

Split composite shapes into rectangles!
Area A = 6 × 2 = 12 m²
Area B = 4 × 3 = 12 m²
Total area = 12 + 12 = 24 m²

🤔 Think About It!

An L-shape is made of a 5 × 3 rectangle and a 2 × 2 rectangle. Total area?

Comparing all four garden sections
Page 8 · Comparing Areas — Which Section Is Biggest?
With all four sections designed and calculated, Aisha drew a comparison chart for Mr. Park. It was time to review the work and understand which parts of the garden were largest.

She listed the areas side by side: the vegetable patch was 24 m², the flower bed was 16 m², the pond was 20 m², and the path was 24 m².

"The vegetable patch and the path are tied for the biggest sections — both 24 square metres," Mr. Park observed, studying the bar chart. "And the flower bed is the smallest."

Ben had one more calculation to do. "If we want to know the total area of the whole garden, we simply add all four sections together," he said. "24 + 16 + 20 + 24." He reached for his pencil.
📊 Comparing Areas

Vegetable patch = Path = 24 m² (tied!)
Pond = 20 m², Flower bed = 16 m²
Read bar charts by checking the height of each bar against the scale.

Page 9
12 tiles for a 4 m × 3 m room
Page 9 · Real-World Area — Tiles, Turf, and Carpet
As the garden project neared its end, Mr. Park had one more question. He was also renovating the school's reading room and needed to know how many floor tiles to order.

The room measured 4 metres wide and 3 metres long. Each tile was exactly 1 square metre. Aisha explained: "To find how many tiles you need, find the area of the floor. Area = 4 × 3 = 12 m². So you need 12 tiles — one for every square metre."

"Area is incredibly useful in real life," Aisha said. "When you lay turf in a garden, order carpet for a room, or buy tiles for a floor, you always need to calculate area first."

Ben nodded thoughtfully. "And if we needed to paint a wall, we'd calculate the wall's area to find out how much paint to buy!" Calculating area, they both agreed, was one of the most practical skills in mathematics.
🏠 Area in the Real World

Area tells us how much carpet, turf, or tiles we need.
A 4 m × 3 m room needs 12 tiles that are each 1 m².
Floor area = number of 1 m² tiles required.

🤔 Think About It!

A wall is 5 m × 2 m. Tins of paint cover 5 m² each. How many tins are needed?

Page 10 · The Garden Is Built!
Weeks later, standing in the sunshine beside the newly completed school garden, Aisha and Ben smiled with pride. The vegetable patch was bursting with green shoots, the flower bed blazed with colour, the pond shimmered peacefully, and the path curved elegantly between each section.

Mr. Park walked through the garden slowly, taking it all in. "This is everything I imagined and more," he said. "And it was all made possible because you knew how to measure space."

Aisha turned to Ben. "Area tells us how much space is inside a shape — it is what we use when we plant, build, and design. Perimeter tells us the distance around a shape — essential for fencing and framing."

"Both are essential," Ben agreed. "Every architect, builder, and gardener uses area and perimeter every single day." The two friends shook hands, knowing they had done excellent work.
📝 What You Have Learned

Area = space inside a shape (L × W, in m² or cm²)
Perimeter = distance around a shape (2 × (L + W))
Composite shapes: split into rectangles and add areas.
Both are essential for building, designing, and planning!
