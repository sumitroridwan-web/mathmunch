Page 2 · The Case Begins
Detective Dara arrived at the Maths Museum early one morning. Her assistant Theo was already there, looking worried.

"Dara! Someone has stolen numbers from the 100-square!" he cried, pointing at the giant grid on the wall.

Sure enough, several numbers had been replaced by question marks. Dara pulled out her magnifying glass.

"A 100-square shows every number from 1 to 100," she said calmly. "Each row has 10 numbers, going left to right."

Theo nodded. "And each column goes downwards from top to bottom!"

Dara smiled. "Exactly. And that is how we will catch the thief — by using the patterns!"

She took out her detective notebook and got to work.
Every number on the 100-square has a special position. Once we know the pattern, we can find any missing number!
🔢 Maths Fact: The 100-Square

A 100-square shows numbers 1–100 in rows of 10. Rows go across (left to right). Columns go down (top to bottom).

Page 3 · Row Patterns
Dara began examining the 100-square row by row, moving her magnifying glass from left to right.

"Look, Theo!" she whispered excitedly. "Every time you move one step to the right, the number gets one bigger!"

Theo picked up a green pencil and drew arrows. "21, 22, 23, 24, 25... it goes up by 1 every single time!"

"And going the other way — moving LEFT — the number gets one smaller," Dara added. "That is −1 each step."

The thief had stolen numbers from several places in the rows. But now Dara could find them.

She just needed to look at the number before or after the gap, and count one step!

"These row patterns are clue number one," she told Theo, writing it in her notebook.
Every step to the right on the 100-square adds exactly 1. Every step to the left takes 1 away. Simple!
➡️ Pattern: Going Across Rows

Going RIGHT on the 100-square means +1 each step. Going LEFT means −1.

Column 5: going down adds 10
Column 5: 5, 15, 25, 35... (+10 each step!)
Page 4 · Column Patterns
Next, Dara turned her attention to the columns — the vertical lines going down the 100-square.

"Theo, look at column 5," she said, tracing her finger downward. "5, 15, 25, 35, 45..."

"Each number is 10 more than the one above it!" Theo exclaimed.

"Precisely," said Dara. "Going DOWN on the 100-square always adds 10."

"And going UP takes 10 away — it's −10," she continued, moving her finger upward.

This was clue number two. The thief had stolen numbers from the middle of several columns.

But Dara just needed to find the number above or below the gap, then add or subtract 10!
Going down one step on the 100-square always adds 10. That is why column 5 has 5, 15, 25, 35, 45, 55, 65, 75, 85, 95!
⬇️ Pattern: Going Down Columns

Going DOWN on the 100-square means +10 each step. Going UP means −10.

Diagonal clues from the thief!
Diagonal pattern: +11 going down-right!
Page 5 · Diagonal Clues
Dara found something unexpected — the thief had left clues arranged diagonally across the 100-square.

"A diagonal means going down AND across at the same time," Dara explained to Theo.

She traced her finger from 11 to 22 to 33 to 44. "Look! Each step goes one row down and one column right."

"Down is +10, right is +1, so diagonally down-right is... +11!" said Theo.

"Excellent detective work!" Dara said proudly. "And going down-left would be +10 and −1, which is +9."

These diagonal patterns were how the thief had been hiding clues. But now Dara could read them.

She circled the diagonal numbers in her notebook: 11, 22, 33, 44, 55, 66. Each one exactly 11 apart.
Diagonal clues! Going diagonally down and to the right adds 11 each time. Down and left adds 9. The grid never lies!
↘️ Pattern: Diagonal Moves

Going diagonally down-right = +11 each step. Going diagonally down-left = +9 each step.

Page 6 · Even and Odd Patterns
Dara stepped back and looked at the whole 100-square with fresh eyes. Something beautiful appeared.

"Theo! The numbers alternate — even, odd, even, odd — all the way across every single row!"

Even numbers end in 0, 2, 4, 6 or 8. Odd numbers end in 1, 3, 5, 7 or 9.

Dara coloured the evens blue and the odds orange. A perfect striped pattern appeared.

"Look at column 2," she said. "2, 12, 22, 32, 42 — all even! The whole column is blue!"

Theo checked column 1: "1, 11, 21, 31, 41 — all odd! All orange!"

The thief had tried to mix up even and odd numbers — but the pattern gave them away.
Even numbers end in 0, 2, 4, 6 or 8. Odd numbers end in 1, 3, 5, 7 or 9. On the 100-square, they always alternate!
🔵 Pattern: Even and Odd Numbers

Even numbers (2,4,6,8,10...) alternate with odd numbers (1,3,5,7,9...) across every row. They never sit next to one another!

Multiples of 5 make two green columns!
Page 7 · Skip Counting Patterns
Dara had one more pattern to find. She picked up a green highlighter and started marking numbers.

"I am going to mark every multiple of 5," she said. "Numbers I land on when I count in fives."

5... 10... 15... 20... 25... 30... She marked them all in bright green.

When she stepped back, two entire columns were glowing green — the column ending in 5 and the column ending in 0!

"Multiples of 5 always end in 5 or 0," she explained to Theo. "So they always fall in those two columns."

Theo grabbed the green pen. "42 does NOT end in 5 or 0 — so it is not a multiple of 5!"

Dara nodded. "Exactly. These patterns are how we prove a number belongs — or does not belong!"
Multiples of 5 always end in 5 or 0. On the 100-square, they form two neat columns — one for 5s and one for 0s!
5️⃣ Pattern: Multiples of 5

Multiples of 5 always end in 5 or 0. They make a column pattern on the 100-square: 5, 15, 25, 35... and 10, 20, 30, 40...

Page 8 · The Missing Numbers!
With all four patterns in her notebook, Dara was ready to find every single missing number.

"Row pattern — add 1 going right," she said firmly. "Column pattern — add 10 going down."

She moved methodically across the grid, filling in each question mark.

For a number missing from the middle of a row: look left, add 1. Done!

For a number missing from the middle of a column: look above, add 10. Done!

Theo watched in amazement as Dara filled in six missing numbers in under a minute.

"Every number has a proper place," Dara said with a satisfied smile. "The patterns never lie."
Use rows (+1), columns (+10), and your knowledge of patterns. Once you understand the 100-square, no number can stay hidden for long!

Page 9 · Case Closed!
With the last missing number restored, the 100-square glowed and every number settled back into place.

Detective Dara had done it — she had used the four patterns to catch the thief's trick and fix the entire grid.

Theo clapped and cheered. "You found them all! How did you do it so fast?"

"Patterns," said Dara simply. "The 100-square is full of them — rows, columns, diagonals, even and odd, multiples."

The museum director rushed in. "You have saved the 100-square! The thief has been caught trying to sneak back!"

Dara and Theo smiled at each other. Another maths mystery solved — with the power of number patterns.

"The 100-square never lies," Dara said, putting away her magnifying glass. "As long as you know the patterns."
