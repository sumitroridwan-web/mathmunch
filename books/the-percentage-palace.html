Page 2 · What is a Percentage?
The grand Percentage Palace sat at the heart of the kingdom of Centuria, its walls decorated with the symbol % — the crest of the royal house. Every financial decision in Centuria was made using percentages, and every citizen understood their language fluently.

Chancellor Clio, the kingdom's chief financial advisor, had been appointed to manage the annual royal budget. The treasury held one hundred gold pieces, and every department of the palace — the Royal Guards, the kitchens, the repair crews, the entertainment guild, and the savings vault — demanded their share.

Before Clio could allocate a single coin, the Chancellor needed to ensure every minister understood the fundamental concept: what is a percentage?

"Percentage literally means 'per hundred' — from the Latin per centum," Clio explained to the assembled ministers, gesturing to a 10 × 10 grid of squares on the wall. "When we say 25%, we mean 25 out of every 100. I have shaded 25 squares on this grid of 100. Those 25 squares represent exactly 25% of the whole."

"The % symbol is shorthand for this relationship: any number followed by % tells you how many parts out of 100 we are describing."
💯 What is a Percentage?

PERCENTAGE means "out of 100". 25% = 25/100 = 0.25. The % symbol means "per cent" (Latin: per centum = per hundred).

Page 3 · Percentages to Fractions
The first task on Chancellor Clio's agenda was to ensure all ministers could convert percentages to fractions. Many of the palace's historical records expressed quantities as fractions, so fluency in conversion was essential for comparing old records with current percentage-based budgets.

"The conversion is entirely systematic," Clio said, writing clearly on the ledger board. "To convert a percentage to a fraction, you write the percentage value as the numerator over the denominator 100, then simplify the resulting fraction by dividing both numerator and denominator by their Highest Common Factor."

"Example: 75% becomes 75/100. The HCF of 75 and 100 is 25. Dividing both by 25: 75 ÷ 25 = 3 and 100 ÷ 25 = 4. Therefore 75% = 3/4."

"Example: 80% becomes 80/100. HCF of 80 and 100 is 20. 80 ÷ 20 = 4 and 100 ÷ 20 = 5. Therefore 80% = 4/5." Clio displayed the conversion table prominently so every minister could reference it throughout the budget session.

The ministers who recognised these fraction equivalents immediately held a significant advantage in mental arithmetic — they could estimate budget shares without a calculator.
🔄 Percentage → Fraction

% → Fraction: write over 100, then simplify. 75% = 75/100 = 3/4. 80% = 80/100 = 4/5. Always simplify using the HCF.

Page 4 · Percentages to Decimals
The palace's accounting system used both percentages and decimals in its financial records. Chancellor Clio needed every minister to be able to convert percentages to decimals instantly, because the treasury's calculating machines worked exclusively with decimal inputs.

"The conversion rule is beautifully simple," Clio announced. "To convert a percentage to a decimal, divide by 100. In practical terms, this means moving the decimal point two places to the left."

"Consider 35%. Dividing by 100: 35 ÷ 100 = 0.35. The decimal point moves from after the 35 to between the 0 and 3."

"A particularly important case is 7%. New mathematicians often write this as 0.7, which is incorrect — 0.7 = 70%. The correct answer is 0.07, with a zero holding the tenths place. Always ensure you move the decimal exactly two places." Clio demonstrated this carefully on the ledger board, writing each digit precisely.

"And 100% ÷ 100 = 1.0, confirming that 100% represents one complete whole — which aligns perfectly with our understanding of 100 out of 100."
Worked Example: Convert 8% to a decimal
1Write 8% as a division: 8 ÷ 100
2Move decimal point 2 places left: 8. → 0.08
3Check: the zero in the tenths place is essential
4Answer: 8% = 0.08
🔢 Percentage → Decimal

% → Decimal: divide by 100 (move decimal point 2 places left). 35% = 0.35. 7% = 0.07. 100% = 1.0.

Page 5 · The Conversion Triangle
Chancellor Clio now brought together all three forms of expressing parts of a whole: fractions, decimals, and percentages. These three representations are entirely equivalent — they describe the same quantity using different notation systems. Mastering the conversions between them is one of the most powerful skills in numeracy.

"Think of these three forms as three languages that say the same thing," Clio explained. "Three quarters can be written as the fraction ¾, as the decimal 0.75, or as the percentage 75%. All three mean exactly the same thing: three out of every four equal parts."

"The conversion triangle shows the pathways: from fraction to decimal, divide the numerator by the denominator (3 ÷ 4 = 0.75). From decimal to percentage, multiply by 100 (0.75 × 100 = 75%). From percentage to decimal, divide by 100 (75% ÷ 100 = 0.75). From decimal to fraction, write the decimal as a fraction over a power of 10 and simplify."

Clio demonstrated with two more examples: ½ = 0.5 = 50%, and ⅕ = 0.2 = 20%. The ministers studied the conversion triangle pinned to the Treasury wall with growing confidence.
🔺 The Conversion Triangle

The triple link: Fraction ↔ Decimal ↔ Percentage. Know one, find the other two. ¾ = 0.75 = 75%.

Page 6 · Finding Percentages of Amounts
The first real budget allocation task arrived on Chancellor Clio's desk. The Royal Guard requested 25% of the treasury's 200-gold-piece reserve to fund their annual equipment maintenance. Clio needed to calculate 25% of 200 exactly.

"There are two reliable methods for finding a percentage of an amount," Clio explained to the junior budget clerks. "The formula method: multiply the amount by the percentage expressed as a decimal or fraction. 25% of 200 = 200 × 0.25 = 50. Or: 200 × 25/100 = 50."

"The 10% method — useful for mental arithmetic: find 10% first by dividing by 10. 10% of 200 = 20. Then build up: 25% = 2 × 10% + 5%. 5% = half of 10% = 10. So 25% = 20 + 20 + 10 = 50."

"Both methods give the same answer: £50 for the Royal Guard." Clio recorded this in the budget ledger with precise handwriting. The junior clerks copied down both methods, recognising that the 10% method would be especially valuable when they didn't have access to pen and paper.
💰 Finding Percentages of Amounts

10% method: find 10% (÷10), then multiply. 25% of 200: 10% = 20, 25% = 20 × 2.5 = 50. Or: 200 × 25/100 = 50.

Page 7 · Percentage Increase
The Royal Guards had performed exemplary service throughout the year. The monarch decreed that every guard's salary should be increased by 15%. Chancellor Clio needed to calculate the new salary for a guard currently earning £400 per month.

"A percentage increase involves two steps," Clio explained methodically. "Step 1: calculate the percentage of the original amount. Step 2: add that amount to the original." This two-step process is essential and should never be conflated into a single operation without care.

"Step 1: 15% of £400. Using the 10% method: 10% of 400 = 40. 5% = half of that = 20. 15% = 40 + 20 = £60."

"Step 2: New salary = original + increase = £400 + £60 = £460."

"Alternatively, using the multiplier method: a 15% increase means the new amount is 115% of the original. 115% as a decimal is 1.15. New salary = 400 × 1.15 = 460. Both methods give the same answer." Clio recorded the new salary in the guards' payroll ledger and moved on to the next budget item.
Worked Example: £400 increased by 15%
1Find 15% of £400: 10% = £40, 5% = £20, so 15% = £60
2Add to original: £400 + £60 = £460
3Check: 400 × 1.15 = £460 ✓
📈 Percentage Increase

% Increase: find the % of the original, then ADD it. 15% of 400 = 60. New amount = 400 + 60 = 460.

Page 8 · Percentage Decrease
The kingdom's harvest had been smaller than expected, requiring Chancellor Clio to implement a 10% reduction in the royal kitchen's food budget. The current food budget stood at £500. Clio needed to calculate the reduced amount that would remain after the cut was applied.

"A percentage decrease mirrors the increase process exactly, but in the opposite direction," Clio explained to the kitchen minister. "Step 1: calculate the percentage of the original. Step 2: subtract that amount from the original."

"Step 1: 10% of £500. Dividing by 10: 10% = £50."

"Step 2: Remaining budget = £500 − £50 = £450."

"Using the multiplier method: a 10% decrease means we retain 90% of the original. 90% as a decimal = 0.9. New budget = 500 × 0.9 = 450. Consistent with our previous answer." Clio noted that the kitchen minister looked relieved — a 10% cut was significant but not catastrophic. The remaining £450 would still allow the palace kitchens to operate at full service if careful planning was applied.
📉 Percentage Decrease

% Decrease: find the % of the original, then SUBTRACT it. 10% of 500 = 50. New amount = 500 − 50 = 450.

Page 9 · Comparing Percentages
With all individual allocations calculated, Chancellor Clio now presented the complete royal budget to the court. The budget was displayed as a pie chart — a circular diagram divided into sectors, each representing one department's percentage share of the total treasury.

"The fundamental rule of any budget expressed in percentages is this," Clio declared: "All percentages must sum to exactly 100%. This is not optional — it is a mathematical certainty, because 100% represents the complete whole."

"Our five departments receive: Royal Guards — 30%, Royal Kitchen — 25%, Palace Repairs — 20%, Entertainment Guild — 15%, Treasury Savings — 10%. Let us verify: 30 + 25 + 20 + 15 + 10 = 100. The budget is balanced."

"If any figure in a percentage breakdown does not sum to 100, an error has occurred somewhere — either a department has been omitted, or a figure has been calculated incorrectly." Clio demonstrated this verification process twice, showing that the arithmetic check was as important as the allocation itself.

The court applauded. Every minister could see their department's share clearly represented in the pie chart — a powerful visual representation of proportional allocation.
🥧 Percentages in a Pie Chart

All percentages in a pie chart must add to 100%. 30 + 25 + 20 + 15 + 10 = 100. Always check they sum to 100!

Page 10 · Budget Approved!
Chancellor Clio stood before the full royal court, the completed budget ledger open on the presentation stand. Every minister, every guard captain, every guild representative and palace official had assembled to hear the final budget announcement.

"I present to the court the annual Budget of Centuria," Clio began. "Every allocation has been calculated with mathematical precision using percentages, and the total — as required by law and by arithmetic — sums to exactly 100% of the royal treasury."

The monarch studied the figures carefully and nodded. "This is exemplary work, Chancellor. The Fraction Kingdom and the Coordinate Compass have nothing on the precision of your budget mathematics."

Clio bowed gracefully. "The secret, Your Majesty, lies in understanding that percentages, fractions, and decimals are three languages describing the same truth. ½ is 50% is 0.5. ¼ is 25% is 0.25. Once you can move fluently between them, and once you can calculate percentage increases, decreases, and amounts — no budget in any kingdom is beyond your grasp."

The court applauded warmly, and the budget was officially approved. Centuria's finances were in the most capable mathematical hands in the realm.
