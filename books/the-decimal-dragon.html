Page 2 · What Is a Decimal?
Deep in the Mountains of Numbers lived Dragon Decimo, a magnificent dragon who guarded the legendary Decimal Vault. The vault held treasures of great mathematical knowledge, but it was sealed with nine special keys.

One bright morning, a young scholar named Sage arrived at the mountain. Sage had studied hard at the Academy of Numbers and believed that understanding decimals was the only way to unlock the vault.

Decimo peered down with glowing golden eyes and said, "To earn the first key, you must show me you understand what a decimal truly is."

Sage took a deep breath and studied the diagram carved into the mountain wall. It showed a whole rectangle divided into ten equal parts, with just one part coloured in blue.
Every whole number hides tiny parts between it and the next! I call them decimals — and I guard their secrets carefully.
💡 Maths Word: Decimal

A decimal uses a DECIMAL POINT to show parts of a whole.
0.1 = one tenth = 1/10
The digits before the point are whole numbers; the digits after show parts of a whole.

Page 3 · Tenths — The First Decimal Place
Decimo was impressed. Sage had earned the first key! The dragon led Sage deeper into the mountain, where a glowing chart was carved into the wall — a place value chart showing ones and tenths.

"Every number has a home," Decimo explained. "The digits before the decimal point live in the ones, tens, and hundreds columns. The first digit after the decimal point lives in the tenths column."

Sage looked carefully at the number 2.3 on the chart. The digit 2 sat in the ones column, and the digit 3 sat in the tenths column. It made perfect sense — 2.3 was two whole units and three-tenths of another unit.

"Imagine a ruler," Sage thought. "Between 2 cm and 3 cm, there are ten equal spaces. Each space is one tenth — or 0.1!"
📊 Tenths: The First Decimal Place

The first decimal place is TENTHS.
2.3 means 2 ones and 3 tenths.
Think of it as 2 + 3/10.

Page 4 · Hundredths — The Second Decimal Place
The third key required even more precision. Decimo unveiled a giant glowing grid — ten rows and ten columns of tiny squares, making one hundred in total.

"Each tiny square," Decimo said, "is one hundredth of the whole. We write it as 0.01. If I shade one square, I have shaded 0.01 of the whole grid."

Sage counted carefully. Decimo then shaded 35 squares. "Now what do I have?" the dragon asked, smoke rising from its nostrils.

Sage thought: 35 squares out of 100. That is 35 hundredths. In the tenths column there are 3 tenths, and in the hundredths column there are 5 hundredths. So the decimal is 0.35.

Decimo roared with delight, and a golden key appeared on the cave wall.
💯 Hundredths: The Second Decimal Place

The second decimal place is HUNDREDTHS.
0.01 = one hundredth = 1/100.
Think of a 10×10 grid: one tiny square = 0.01.
0.35 = 3 tenths + 5 hundredths = 35 hundredths.

Page 5 · Reading and Writing Decimals
At the entrance to the vault's inner chamber, Sage found a combination lock. But instead of numbers, it showed the decimal 4.67 carved in glowing stone. Decimo landed beside Sage and studied the number.

"This is the vault combination," Decimo said in a deep, rumbling voice. "But to enter, you must tell me what each digit means. Not just what it looks like — what it truly represents."

Sage studied the three separate boxes that appeared in the air: one for each digit. The 4 glowed in the ones column. The 6 lit up in the tenths column. The 7 shone in the hundredths column.

Sage understood: 4.67 is not just four and sixty-seven. It is four ones, six tenths, and seven hundredths. Each digit lives in a specific column, and its place tells us its value.
To unlock my vault, you must read 4.67 correctly. Tell me — what does each digit truly mean?
🔑 Place Value in 4.67

4.67: the 4 is in the ones place, 6 is in the tenths place, 7 is in the hundredths place.
4.67 = 4 + 0.6 + 0.07

Page 6 · Ordering Decimals
Four vault keys lay scattered across the cave floor, each engraved with a decimal number: 0.45, 0.7, 0.2, and 0.85. Decimo explained that the keys had to be placed in order from smallest to largest to open the next door.

"How do we order decimals?" Sage asked, picking up each heavy golden key.

Decimo drew a number line on the cave wall, stretching from 0 to 1. "Always compare the tenths digit first," the dragon explained. "The key with the fewest tenths goes first. If two numbers have the same tenths digit, then we look at the hundredths digit to decide."

Sage arranged the keys carefully: 0.2, then 0.45, then 0.7, and finally 0.85. The door clicked open with a satisfying clunk, and cool air rushed from the vault.
📏 Ordering Decimals

To order decimals: compare tenths first, then hundredths.
0.45 < 0.5 because 4 tenths < 5 tenths.
Write them on a number line to see their order clearly.

Page 7 · Rounding Decimals
The next challenge surprised Sage. Decimo produced a long scroll of numbers — measurements of the vault's treasures — and said, "These numbers are too precise. I need you to round them to one decimal place."

"Rounding?" Sage said. "I know this rule!"

The dragon nodded slowly. "When rounding to one decimal place, you look at the second decimal place — the hundredths digit. If it is 5 or greater, you round the tenths digit up by one. If it is less than 5, you keep the tenths digit the same."

Sage studied the number 2.36 on the scroll. The hundredths digit was 6 — that is greater than or equal to 5 — so the tenths digit of 3 rounds up to 4. The answer is 2.4.

Decimo blew a small satisfied puff of smoke and handed Sage another golden key.
🔄 Rounding to 1 Decimal Place

Look at the hundredths digit.
If it is 5 or more, round up the tenths digit.
If it is less than 5, keep the tenths digit the same.

Page 8 · Adding Decimals
Deep inside the vault, Sage found two golden bars weighing 2.35 kg and 1.42 kg. To open the penultimate door, Sage needed to calculate their total weight.

"Adding decimals is just like adding whole numbers," Decimo explained, "but you must always line up the decimal points in a column. If the decimal points are not aligned, your answer will be wrong."

Sage wrote the calculation carefully in columns: ones, decimal point, tenths, hundredths. Starting from the right — add the hundredths: 5 + 2 = 7. Then the tenths: 3 + 4 = 7. Then the ones: 2 + 1 = 3. The decimal point stays in the same position.

2.35 + 1.42 = 3.77 — and the door swung open. Sage was nearly at the heart of the vault.
➕ Adding Decimals

Always line up the decimal points when adding!
2.35 + 1.42: add hundredths (7), tenths (7), ones (3) → 3.77.
Work column by column, right to left.

Page 9 · Real-World Decimals
At last, Sage reached the deepest chamber of the Decimal Vault. Rather than gold or jewels, the vault was filled with something far more valuable: examples of decimals used in the real world.

"Decimals are not just for maths lessons," Decimo said proudly. "They are everywhere you look."

Sage gazed around the chamber. There was a price tag showing £3.75 — three pounds and seventy-five hundredths of a pound. There was a ruler showing 15.6 cm — fifteen centimetres and six tenths. There was a gymnastics scoreboard showing 9.4 out of 10 — nine and four tenths.

Sage smiled with understanding. Every time someone uses money, measures an object, or checks a sports score, they are reading and writing decimals. The vault contained the whole world's relationship with this important idea.
🌍 Decimals in the Real World

Decimals are everywhere:
Money: £3.75 = 3 pounds and 75p (75 hundredths)
Length: 15.6 cm = 15 cm and 6 tenths
Scores: 9.4 = nine and four tenths

Page 10 · The Vault is Open!
With all nine keys in hand, Sage approached the great door of the Decimal Vault. One by one, each key turned in its lock with a golden click. The door swung open with a majestic rumble, and warm light flooded out.

Inside was not gold — it was something even more precious: perfect understanding. Numbers floated in the air like fireflies: 0.1, 0.35, 4.67, 3.77 — each one meaningful, each one part of a pattern that made the world a more precise and beautiful place.

Decimo landed beside Sage, and for the first time, the great dragon smiled. "You have proven yourself worthy," Decimo said. "You understand what decimals truly are — not just symbols, but a language for describing the parts of our world."

From that day on, Sage and Decimo became the greatest of friends — a scholar and a dragon who guarded the secrets of decimals together, and welcomed any young mathematician who came to learn.
