Raw data: City temperatures over 6 days
Dr Vera and Dr Rex collect city temperature data
Page 2 · What Is Statistics?
Dr Vera and Dr Rex were data scientists at the City Research Institute. Their lab was filled with whiteboards, spreadsheets, and the gentle hum of computers running calculations. One morning, the mayor sent them an urgent message: temperatures across the city had been behaving strangely, and nobody could explain why.

"Before we can solve any mystery," said Dr Vera, pulling up a fresh spreadsheet, "we must collect and organise our data properly. That is the first rule of statistics."

Statistics is the science of collecting, organising, and interpreting numerical data to draw meaningful conclusions. Dr Rex began recording the daily high temperature for the city — six days of raw, unprocessed numbers sitting in a table.
📋 Key Concept: Raw Data

Raw data is information that has been collected but not yet organised or analysed. The temperature data set is: {18, 22, 18, 25, 22, 30}. To begin drawing conclusions, statisticians first order the data from smallest to largest — this is called an ordered data set.

Page 3 · The Mode
Dr Vera turned to a second data set — temperature anomalies recorded at six sensor stations: {3, 5, 3, 7, 3, 5}. "Let us look for patterns," she said, drawing a tally chart on the whiteboard. "The number that appears most frequently is called the mode."

Counting each value: 3 appears three times, 5 appears twice, and 7 appears once. Therefore the mode is 3. Dr Rex noted that some data sets can have two modes — called bimodal — if two values share the highest frequency. A data set with no repeating value has no mode.
🔢 The Mode — Most Frequent Value

The mode is the value that occurs most often in a data set.
Data: {3, 5, 3, 7, 3, 5}
Tally: 3 → |||, 5 → ||, 7 → |
Mode = 3 (appears 3 times)

A bimodal set has two modes, e.g. {2, 4, 2, 6, 4} → modes are 2 and 4.

Odd sets: one middle value; even sets: average the two middles
Page 4 · The Median
"The mode tells us what is most popular," said Dr Rex, "but it does not always represent a typical value." He introduced the second measure of average — the median — which is the middle value when all data is arranged in order from smallest to largest.

For an odd number of values, the median is simply the middle item. For an even number of values, there is no single middle — instead, you find the mean of the two middle values. With the ordered set {5, 8, 12, 16, 20, 24}, the two middle values are 12 and 16, so the median is (12 + 16) ÷ 2 = 14. The median is useful because it is not pulled by extreme values the way a mean can be.
📉 The Median — Middle Value

Step 1: Order the data set smallest → largest.
Step 2 (odd count): pick the middle value.
Step 2 (even count): add the two middle values and divide by 2.

Example: {3, 7, 9, 11, 15} → median = 9
Example: {4, 6, 10, 14} → median = (6 + 10) ÷ 2 = 8

Page 5 · The Mean
Dr Vera introduced the most widely used average — the arithmetic mean. "Imagine you could redistribute all the values equally among every item in the data set," she explained. "The amount each item would receive is the mean."

To calculate the mean, add all the values together, then divide by the number of values. For the temperature data set {12, 8, 15, 10, 5}: the sum is 12 + 8 + 15 + 10 + 5 = 50, and there are 5 values, so the mean is 50 ÷ 5 = 10°C. The mean is a powerful tool but can be skewed by extreme outliers, which is why statisticians always consider all three averages together.
📈 The Mean — Sum ÷ Count

Mean = (sum of all values) ÷ (number of values)

Data: {12, 8, 15, 10, 5}
Sum = 12 + 8 + 15 + 10 + 5 = 50
Count = 5
Mean = 50 ÷ 5 = 10

Range measures the spread of a data set
Page 6 · The Range
"Averages tell us about the centre of data," said Dr Rex, "but they say nothing about the spread." He introduced the range — a measure of how spread out a data set is. The range is calculated simply: highest value minus lowest value.

Comparing two data sets with the same mean can be misleading if their ranges differ dramatically. Set A has values {10, 14, 16, 18, 20} with a range of 10, while Set B has values {5, 14, 16, 18, 30} with a range of 25. Both sets have a mean of 16, yet Set B's data is far more spread out — it is less consistent and more unpredictable, which is crucial information for any data scientist.
↔️ The Range — Highest Minus Lowest

Range = highest value − lowest value

Set A: {10, 14, 16, 18, 20} → Range = 20 − 10 = 10
Set B: {5, 14, 16, 18, 30} → Range = 30 − 5 = 25

A small range means data is consistent. A large range means data is spread out and variable.

🤔 Think About It!

Choosing the right average for your data
Page 7 · Choosing the Right Average
"Statistics would be straightforward if one average always worked," said Dr Vera, "but in the real world, context matters enormously." Each of the three averages has particular strengths and weaknesses that a skilled data scientist must understand.

Consider a shoe shop owner who wants to know which size to stock most. The mode is the perfect choice — it reveals the most common shoe size purchased. For salaries at a company where one executive earns £500,000 while most staff earn £25,000, the median is fairer — the mean would be heavily distorted by that single extreme salary, an outlier. The mean works best when data is evenly spread without extreme values pulling it in one direction.
💡 Which Average to Choose?

Mode — best for categories and popularity (shoe sizes, favourite colours).
Median — best when data has outliers (incomes, house prices).
Mean — best for evenly spread numerical data (test scores, temperatures).

An outlier is a value that is much larger or smaller than the rest of the data.

Seven people's weekly earnings: {£200, £210, £215, £220, £225, £230, £2000}. The £2000 is an outlier. Which average best represents a typical weekly earning?

Frequency tables organise large data sets efficiently
Page 8 · Frequency Tables
Back at the lab, Dr Rex had collected data from 16 weather sensors, each reporting a storm severity score from 1 to 5. "With this many readings," he said, "a raw list becomes unwieldy. A frequency table organises the data clearly."

A frequency table records each possible value and how many times it appears — the frequency. To calculate the mean from a frequency table, multiply each value by its frequency (f × x), sum all those products, then divide by the total frequency. This technique is far more efficient than listing every single data point and is essential for large data sets used in professional research.
📊 Mean from a Frequency Table

Mean = Σ(f × x) ÷ Σf
where Σ means "sum of all".

From the table: Σ(f × x) = 2+8+18+12+5 = 45
Total frequency Σf = 2+4+6+3+1 = 16
Mean = 45 ÷ 16 = 2.8125 ≈ 2.8

Grouped bar charts compare two data sets at once
Page 9 · Bar Charts and Histograms
Dr Vera pinned a grouped bar chart to the lab wall, displaying monthly rainfall data for two cities. "Visual representations of data," she explained, "allow us to identify patterns and trends far more rapidly than staring at raw numbers." A well-constructed chart communicates in seconds what a table might take minutes to convey.

In a grouped bar chart, two or more bars are placed side by side within each category, enabling direct comparison. Reading such charts requires attention to the key or legend, the scale on the vertical axis, and the labels along the horizontal axis. Dr Rex noted that City A had its highest rainfall in March, while City B's peak was in April — a critical difference that would affect flood-risk assessments.
📊 Reading Bar Charts

Steps to interpret a bar chart:
1. Read the title — what data is shown?
2. Check the y-axis scale — what do the heights represent?
3. Read each bar height accurately.
4. Look for the highest, lowest, and trends.
5. Compare groups using the legend/key.

Same mean but very different ranges — statistics reveals the full story
Page 10 · Comparing Two Data Sets
Dr Rex placed the test results from two school classes side by side. "This," he said, "is where statistics becomes truly powerful — not just describing one data set, but comparing two to draw meaningful conclusions." Both classes had a mean score of 65, which might suggest identical performance. But the ranges told a very different story.

Class X had scores {55, 60, 65, 70, 75} with a range of only 20 — a consistent, reliable group. Class Y had scores {40, 58, 65, 72, 90} with a range of 50 — a wide spread indicating some very strong and some struggling students. A teacher reading only the mean would miss this crucial difference entirely.
⚖️ Comparing Data Sets

To compare two data sets, calculate and compare:
1. Mean — which group performed better on average?
2. Range — which group is more consistent?
3. Median — what is the typical middle performance?

A smaller range = more consistent. A larger range = more variable.

Statistics reveals the hidden temperature anomaly pattern
Page 11 · The Data Mystery Solved
Dr Vera laid out all the temperature anomaly data from ten monitoring stations: {2, 5, 2, 8, 2, 11, 2, 14, 2, 17}. "Notice anything?" she asked. Dr Rex studied the ordered set carefully. Five stations showed exactly 2°C above normal, while the other five showed 5, 8, 11, 14, and 17 — increasing by 3°C each time.

The mode was 2°C — misleadingly suggesting most stations were normal. The mean was 6.5°C — inflated by the high outliers. But the median was 3.5°C — and the range of 15°C revealed the dramatic spread. "There is a systematic fault at exactly five stations," declared Dr Vera triumphantly. "Each faulty sensor reads 3°C higher than the previous. Statistics has solved our mystery."
🔍 Full Statistical Analysis

Data: {2, 5, 2, 8, 2, 11, 2, 14, 2, 17}
Ordered: {2, 2, 2, 2, 2, 5, 8, 11, 14, 17}
Mode = 2 (appears 5 times)
Median = (2+5) ÷ 2 = 3.5 (positions 5 and 6)
Mean = 65 ÷ 10 = 6.5
Range = 17 − 2 = 15
